RENDER: Hard animal fat melted and clarified for
other cooking applications.
RICING: Food is pushed or pressured through a
food mill or a ricer to produce a smooth texture,
coarser than a purΘe or passing through a sieve or
tamis but smoother that mashing.
ROAST: Same as baking but temperature is
usually higher.
RONDELLE: Cut food into round thin slices.
ROUGH CHOP: Cut food into uneven pieces.
ROUX: Flour and fat cooked together used to
thicken sauces.
SALTING: Food preservation method which uses
salt, either dry or in a solution to make a brine.
SAUT╔: Food cooked over high heat and left to
brown and develop a fond.
SCALD: Liquid heated until small bubbles start to
form (around 83░C/181░F for milk).
SCORE: Shallow cuts made on the surface of meat,
fish, bread, or cakes. In meat and fish, this helps
better absorption of marinades and in bread and
cakes, for air to escape to avoid steam trapped
inside to blow up and for decoration.
SEAR: Exposing food to high heat to form a brown
flavourful crust.
SEASONING: Enhancing the flavour of food by
adding salt, spices and herbs.
SHALLOW FRY: Food cooked partly submerged in
cooking fat and turned over during cooking to
cook other side(s).
SHUCK: Removal of the outer casing of food, such
as the husk of an ear of corn or the shell of an
oyster.
SIMMER: Food is cooked fully submerged in very
hot liquid, usually water (around 85-95░C). Small
and infrequent bubbles will appear.
SKIM: To remove impurities from the surface of a
liquid, such as a stock or jam, while it is cooking.
SLICE: 1. Food cut lengthwise uniformly. 2. Cutting
technique where the tip of the blade rests on the
cutting board and cuts are made in the forward
and and downward motion.
SMOKE: Food cooked by exposing to smoke from
smouldering material(s), usually wood or wood
chips.
SOUS-VIDE: Food is vacuum-sealed and cooked in
water at a constant temperature. Food such as
steak require a quick sear after sous-vide.
SPATCHCOCK: Split and open a bird, usually a
chicken, by removing the backbone to flatten it
before cooking.
SPHERIFICATION: Liquid shaped into squishy
spheres by using sodium alginate and either
calcium chloride or calcium lactate gluconate.
Spheres resembles fish roe both in appearance
and texture.
STEAK CUT: See PONT NEUF.
STEAMING: Food cooked in steam by placing a
container
with
openings
over
simmering
or
boiling water.
STEEP: Ingredient is soaked in liquid, usually
water, to extract flavours or to soften the item.
STEW: Food may or may not be pan fried or
sautΘed at high temperature and then liquid fully
covering the previous ingredients is added and
cooked slowly.
STIR FRY: Food cooked over very high heat and is
stirred and tossed quickly.
STUFFING: See FILLING.
SUPR╩ME: Term used for the best part of an
ingredient. For example, chicken suprΩme refers
to skin on, boneless chicken breast.
SWEAT: Food cooked over low heat, often with
added salt to draw moisture out without any
browning.
TANDOOR: Cylindrical clay or metal oven used for
cooking and baking.
TEMPER:
1.
Two
liquids
brought
to
similar
temperature before combining them completely,
such as tempering egg to prevent it from cooking
before adding to a hot sauce. 2. Heating and
cooling chocolate to stabilise and give it a smooth
and glossy finish. 3. Spices and other aromatics
briefly fried in oil and ghee to release their flavour
and aroma to be used for cooking or as garnish.
TENDERISE: To make food easier to chew.
THICKEN: Make the liquid or mixture denser by
adding
other
ingredient,
such
as
flour
and
cornstarch, or by reducing it to the desired
viscosity.
ZEST: Outer layer of citrus fruits used as flavouring.
